Slide 6:

What is a fixed mindset?
The idea that your skills are predetermined from birth—that you can't change whether you're good at something or not—is known as a fixed mindset.

What is a growth mindset?
A growth mindset, on the other hand, is the conviction that abilities can be acquired with work, education, and perseverance.

The film tells the tale of László Polgár, who held the opinion that talent is something that is developed via hard work and the correct circumstances rather than something that is innate. He raised his three daughters, Susan, Sophia, and Judit, to be chess champions as evidence of this. All three were successful; Judit even defeated world champion Garry Kasparov to become the world's best female chess player. Their accomplishments demonstrated that diligence and commitment are more important than innate ability.

1a. Essay on What Grit and Mindsets Mean to You

Describe the concept of grit and mind-sets and explain their significance to one’s success

I believe that grit is the ability to pursue long-term goals with unrelenting desire and persistence. Even in the face of waning motivation and impending challenges, it is the silent force that keeps up effort over months or years.

The internal beliefs people have about their capacity to learn, develop, and change are referred to as mindsets, especially the difference between a fixed and a growth mindset as defined by Carol Dweck. A growth mindset recognises that intelligence and skill are only beginnings and that perseverance and persistent work are necessary to reach true mastery.

One cannot stress the importance of mindsets and grit in attaining achievement. Grit, or the will to keep going, and a growth-oriented mindset are what really define one's potential, even when talent can open doors. While a growth mindset encourages adaptability and the never-ending quest for knowledge in the face of failure, grit keeps people going when there are no instant rewards.

Explain the relationship between grit and mind-sets

These two forces are mutually reinforcing. Grit is naturally fostered by a growth mentality; we are more inclined to persevere through failure and setbacks when we think that abilities can be developed. On the other hand, gritted people frequently develop a growth mentality with experience, realising that hard work, not natural talent, is what really propels success.

Develop an individual formula/statement for success

My personal formula for success is:

“Resilience in effort, humility in learning, and faith in continuous evolution.”

I believe that success is not a fixed destination but a dynamic journey shaped by persistent striving and an openness to change.

Slide 8: Goal Hierarchy

A system called goal hierarchy aids in dividing big, long-term goals into more achievable, smaller tasks. It starts with high-level objectives, which serve as a representation of your ultimate desires and establish the course for all of your endeavors. These bigger goals are broken down into mid-level objectives, which concentrate on important areas or benchmarks that will enable you to advance. Lastly, low-level goals—the practical, daily activities that propel you forward—support these. This methodical technique keeps goals reachable, motivation high, and focus consistent throughout the process.

2a. Reflection on Learning – Grit Goals

In class, we learned about grit goals using the goal hierarchy model—a framework that breaks down long-term aspirations into manageable, actionable steps. At the top are core goals, driven by passion and purpose. These are supported by mid-level goals, which act as key milestones. At the foundation are low-level goals, consisting of daily or weekly tasks that keep momentum going.

This approach promotes clarity, persistence, and motivation. Unlike the traditional SMART goals I’ve previously used—which focus more on short-term completion—the grit goal hierarchy encourages consistency, resilience, and alignment with a bigger purpose. It turns large ambitions into smaller wins that are more achievable and sustainable.

Before this, I would often set isolated goals with no clear connection to a greater vision. When one goal failed, I’d lose direction entirely. Now, I understand that setbacks are part of the journey. By tying my smaller tasks to a meaningful top-level goal, I stay grounded and better motivated. This mindset shift has helped me view goal setting as a process of growth, not just outcome-based achievements.
