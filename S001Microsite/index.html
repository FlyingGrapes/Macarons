Lesson 2: Fixed vs Growth Mindset

Slide 6:

What is a fixed mindset?
The idea that your skills are predetermined from birth—that you can't change whether you're good at something or not—is known as a fixed mindset.

What is a growth mindset?
A growth mindset, on the other hand, is the conviction that abilities can be acquired with work, education, and perseverance.

The film tells the tale of László Polgár, who held the opinion that talent is something that is developed via hard work and the correct circumstances rather than something that is innate. He raised his three daughters, Susan, Sophia, and Judit, to be chess champions as evidence of this. All three were successful; Judit even defeated world champion Garry Kasparov to become the world's best female chess player. Their accomplishments demonstrated that diligence and commitment are more important than innate ability.

1a. Essay on What Grit and Mindsets Mean to You

Describe the concept of grit and mind-sets and explain their significance to one’s success

I believe that grit is the ability to pursue long-term goals with unrelenting desire and persistence. Even in the face of waning motivation and impending challenges, it is the silent force that keeps up effort over months or years.

The internal beliefs people have about their capacity to learn, develop, and change are referred to as mindsets, especially the difference between a fixed and a growth mindset as defined by Carol Dweck. A growth mindset recognises that intelligence and skill are only beginnings and that perseverance and persistent work are necessary to reach true mastery.

One cannot stress the importance of mindsets and grit in attaining achievement. Grit, or the will to keep going, and a growth-oriented mindset are what really define one's potential, even when talent can open doors. While a growth mindset encourages adaptability and the never-ending quest for knowledge in the face of failure, grit keeps people going when there are no instant rewards.

Explain the relationship between grit and mind-sets

These two forces are mutually reinforcing. Grit is naturally fostered by a growth mentality; we are more inclined to persevere through failure and setbacks when we think that abilities can be developed. On the other hand, gritted people frequently develop a growth mentality with experience, realising that hard work, not natural talent, is what really propels success.

My personal formula for success is:

“Resilience in effort, humility in learning, and faith in continuous evolution.”

I believe that success is not a fixed destination but a dynamic journey shaped by persistent striving and an openness to change.

Slide 8: Goal Hierarchy

Lastly, low-level goals—the practical, daily activities that propel you forward—support these. This methodical technique keeps goals reachable, motivation high, and focus consistent throughout the process.

In class, we learned about grit goals using the goal hierarchy model—a framework that breaks down long-term aspirations into manageable, actionable steps. At the top are core goals, driven by passion and purpose. These are supported by mid-level goals, which act as key milestones. At the foundation are low-level goals, consisting of daily or weekly tasks that keep momentum going.

This approach promotes clarity, persistence, and motivation. Unlike the traditional SMART goals I’ve previously used—which focus more on short-term completion—the grit goal hierarchy encourages consistency, resilience, and alignment with a bigger purpose. It turns large ambitions into smaller wins that are more achievable and sustainable.

Before this, I would often set isolated goals with no clear connection to a greater vision. When one goal failed, I’d lose direction entirely. Now, I understand that setbacks are part of the journey. By tying my smaller tasks to a meaningful top-level goal, I stay grounded and better motivated. This mindset shift has helped me view goal setting as a process of growth, not just outcome-based achievements.

1. What are the common signs and symptoms of stress?

Stress shows up in many forms — sometimes it's loud, like anxiety attacks or snapping at someone, and sometimes it's subtle, like always feeling tired no matter how much I sleep. I personally experience mental fog, overthinking, and an overwhelming sense of "I can’t even start." Physical signs include headaches, tense shoulders, and fidgeting — my body reacts even before my brain realizes I’m under pressure.

A big one for me is decision fatigue — when I’ve had too many things to process, I freeze on even the smallest choices. It’s not just being lazy; it’s like my brain buffer is full and I need to restart. In school or work, this makes even simple tasks feel impossible and leads to procrastination that spirals.

2. What are the possible causes of stress in your life?

Most of my stress comes from a mix of tight deadlines, overlapping responsibilities, and feeling like I have to meet everyone’s expectations. Group projects sometimes add to it, especially when roles aren’t clearly defined. I also stress myself out by trying to do things perfectly the first time — perfectionism and procrastination feed each other.

Sometimes, even when I have time, I still feel stressed — that’s when I know it’s not about the workload, but about the mental pressure I place on myself to not fall behind or look incompetent.

3. How has stress affected the outcome of an activity or task you worked on before?

Stress makes my work worse when I let it pile up. I’ve turned in projects where I knew I could do better, but I ran out of emotional energy to care. On the flip side, sometimes a little pressure gives me focus — but there’s a fine line between helpful pressure and a total mental breakdown.

When I’m stressed, I avoid things — emails, messages, tasks — until they snowball. It becomes harder to get back into the flow because I associate those things with guilt or fear of failing. That emotional buildup ruins the outcome more than the actual task itself.

4. What is eustress, and how can it be helpful?

Eustress is the good kind of stress — the one that energizes you instead of draining you. It’s like the adrenaline you get before a presentation or competition. Instead of panic, you feel challenged — and that challenge pushes you to perform at your best.

In my life, eustress kicks in during hackathons, showcase prep, or solving real-world problems where there's pressure but also excitement. This kind of stress helps me focus better, stay engaged, and actually enjoy the work.

In real life, eustress is what fuels growth. It’s that "this is hard, but I want to do it" feeling. Whether it’s learning a new skill, applying for internships, or presenting ideas to others — eustress helps me step out of my comfort zone while still feeling in control.

5. Identify a personal example where you experienced eustress.

One of the clearest examples of eustress for me was presenting during our final showcase. I was nervous, but I was also proud of what I built. That stress helped me rehearse better, dress up with confidence, and deliver my points with clarity. It felt good to be challenged — and even better to rise to the occasion.

6. What is neustress?

Neustress is a neutral type of stress. It’s information or situations that don’t directly affect me emotionally but still take up mental space. It’s not something I feel deeply — it’s more like mental background noise.

For example, reading about climate change statistics or seeing global layoffs in tech — these don’t affect me right now, but they still trigger quiet anxiety. They sit at the back of my mind like "What if?" thoughts that don’t need to be solved today but still weigh something.

Neustress reminds me how connected I am to the world — news, opinions, trends — they all enter my awareness and sometimes occupy space that I need for myself. It's not always harmful, but it can reduce my ability to focus on what matters now.

It didn’t panic me, but it’s now a whisper I carry when I make career decisions. That’s neustress — not intense, but persistent.

8. What is distress and how does it affect the mind and body?

Distress is the harmful kind of stress — the one that drags you down instead of lifting you. It causes anxiety, exhaustion, and sometimes physical problems like stomach aches or insomnia. For me, distress leads to emotional shutdown — I stop communicating, stop showing up for myself or others.

Long-term distress can make you lose motivation, even for things you care about. It’s not just "feeling tired" — it’s feeling hopeless or stuck in a loop you can’t escape. I’ve been there, especially during exam periods with personal life drama mixed in. The body holds onto that stress even when the crisis is over.

That’s what distress does — it doesn’t just affect performance, it changes how you feel about yourself.

Why is mindfulness important in daily life?

Mindfulness helps us stay grounded in the present. It reduces stress, improves emotional regulation, enhances concentration, and allows us to appreciate simple moments. In our fast-paced lives, mindfulness reconnects us to our inner clarity and peace.
